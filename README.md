Asymmetrical Democratic Equilibrium

A research framework exploring structural asymmetries between decentralised indigenous religious systems and centralised Abrahamic systems within modern democratic nation-states.

This project combines political theory, public choice theory, sociology, anthropology, colonial history, and comparative religion to examine how institutional structures influence democratic outcomes, political mobilisation, electoral bargaining, and long-term civilisational resilience.

Overview

Asymmetrical Democratic Equilibrium investigates how different theological and institutional architectures interact with democratic systems.

The framework focuses on recurring asymmetries including:

Street mobilisation vs institutional dependence
Consolidated vs fragmented electoral blocs
Open-source vs codified theological systems
Institutional resilience under colonial disruption
Ritual investment and collective cohesion

The objective is to move beyond personality-driven political analysis and instead identify the structural mechanics that shape democratic behaviour over time.

Core Research Areas
1. Political Theory

Analysis of mobilisation asymmetry and the relationship between democratic institutions and organised collective pressure.

2. Public Choice Theory

Study of vote-bank efficiency, bloc consolidation, and bargaining leverage in first-past-the-post electoral systems.

3. Moral Philosophy

Comparative examination of decentralised ethical traditions versus codified doctrinal systems.

4. Colonial Institutional History

Research into the resilience and collapse patterns of indigenous and transnational educational institutions.

5. Anthropology & Sociology

Investigation of ritual participation, communal identity formation, and group-boundary reinforcement.

Research Objectives
Formalise structural asymmetries within democratic systems
Develop measurable analytical variables
Connect theological architecture with political outcomes
Examine institutional resilience across historical disruptions
Build interdisciplinary models for comparative civilisational analysis
